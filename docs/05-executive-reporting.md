# Executive Reporting

*Good reporting is concise, consistent, and decision-oriented.*

Executive reporting should provide clarity, not noise. Strong updates focus on what leaders need to know to support the program and make informed decisions.

Executive reporting is not simply a repetition of program status reports. Operational reporting provides detailed information about milestones, workstreams, risks, dependencies, and delivery progress for program teams and program leadership.

Executive reporting serves a different purpose. It distills the most important signals from operational reporting and communicates them in a concise format that helps executives understand program health, major risks, and any decisions or interventions required.

Program leaders review program status, risks, dependencies, and delivery telemetry, then distill the most important developments into concise messages that help executives understand what matters, make decisions, or remove blockers. None of the Executive's direct reports involved in your program/portfolio should be surprised by what you put in a report and send to an Executive.

## Executive Reporting Signals

Effective executive reporting distills operational program information into a small set of decision-oriented signals that help leadership understand program health and intervene when necessary.

| Executive Signal | Why It Matters |
|-----|-----|
| **Executive decisions or actions required** | Enables leadership to make timely decisions or remove blockers affecting delivery. |
| **Overall program status** | Provides a quick view of program health and delivery progress. |
| **Major risks or blockers** | Surfaces issues that may impact milestones, scope, or outcomes. |
| **Impact on business outcomes** | Connects program progress to the strategic goals the initiative supports. |

Executive reporting should remain concise, decision-oriented, and focused on the information leadership needs to maintain confidence in program execution.
